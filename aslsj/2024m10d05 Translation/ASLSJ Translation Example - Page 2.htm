Ken Davis continues his introduction at time 0:03 in the video. He is explaining who he is as the host of this ASL news video.

English conjugations of the verb “to be” (is, am, are, was, were, will be) are not used in ASL. An ASL speaker usually sets up a scene where something was happening in the past, present (default), or future and then says what is happening at that point. This is another case where it is important to remember various scene location setups from previous sentences in the story.

Ken is using a formal, introductory ASL word “I am.” As the hand is waving across the speaker’s entire body, a literal translation would be “this person here.” You would never use this ASL word for personal descriptions like “I am hungry.”

  • Right hand is brushing against his body. This is a depth part of the location that I am leaving out for simplicity along with the horizontal part.
  • Right hand is facing in but the fingers are pointing down. I am also leaving out this finger-pointing spin part.
  • Right hand has four fingers extended forward, all bent at the knuckle. I am also leaving out handshape variations for now.
  • Right hand moves down then back up. I am leaving out repetition / bouncing / double-back movements.

Next Ken spells out his full name: Ken Davis. Fingerspelling of English words is common in ASL. The Deaf community is a small fraction of the total English community, and therefore there are not usually ASL words for specialized English jargon or proper names of people and places.

Fingerspelling is performed very quickly by native ASL speakers. As you are learning, you can ask “Please repeat that” to a Deaf person if you miss something. They would much rather you understand than bluff your way through a conversation.

After spelling his name, he gives the phrase “Deaf Newspaper” as his personality’s title on this YouTube channel. This is different from Ken’s actual name sign, which is a K bouncing from chin to chest.

Ken is now done with introducing the channel and himself. Now he introduces his guest. Although she is literally on his right side in the video, Ken could have focused his attention on an imaginary scene on his right side if she were not there. The drawing above of a boxed-in face shows an arrow pointing right to represent the object of Ken’s next sentence.

This ASL word for “This is” is just a variation on the “I am” introductory word. His hand is gesturing down the whole torso of his guest, literally translated as “that person there.”

At time 0:09, Ken shifts his entire body to face his guest. This means he is speaking directly to Sarah, not just about her.

The ASL adjective “Sign” is different from the ASL verb “Sign”.

  • Right hand in neutral location.
  • Right hand behind left. In the drawing above, the half-circle represents the right hand. The triangle pointing up means the left hand is out / forward from the left hand. The line separating the two means the right hand is not touching the left.
  • Right hand palm out.
  • Right one finger extended.
  • Left palm out. The drawing is flipped from the right hand drawing because the left hand is a mirror image of the right hand when they are both facing the same direction.
  • Left one finger extended. The handshape drawing is not flipped from the right hand, just the palm facing drawing.
  • Right move in a circle around the left, first move down, arc out, then complete the circle back to where it started behind the left hand.
  • Left hand move in a circle around the right hand, first move up, arc in, then complete the circle back to where it started in front of the right. Only the up arrow is shown because this implies both hands have a “following motion” around each other.

ASL word “Name” incorporates the manual alphabet letter N. This is called an initialized ASL word. The handshape represents the initial letter of its corresponding English word. Sometimes there are a group of ASL words that only differ by the handshape to represent the initial letter of several similar English words, like “L” for Law, “C” for Commandment, and “R” for rule.

  • Right hand neutral location.
  • Right hand above left.
  • Right hand neutral palm.
  • Right hand two fingers extended. The manual alphabet letters H, N, and U all have two fingers extended. Initialized ASL words are unique enough that you can figure out which letter the handshape is supposed to represent.
  • Left hand neutral palm.
  • Left hand two fingers extended.
  • Right hand move down. The right hand (dominant hand) in ASL has far more movement than the left hand. In many ASL words, the left hand stays still to represent some prop that the right hand’s action is moving around or striking. In ASL word “Newspaper”, the left hand is a piece of paper. Here the left hand may have originally represented a piece of paper that you were being asked to put your name on, but the left hand’s handshape gradually changed to mirror the right hand.

A “name sign” is culturally important to Deaf people. It is not arbitrary like baby names, but incorporate something about the person’s features or personality. You should not try to create your own sign name or ask for one, but wait until you have interacted with a Deaf person long enough that they choose to give you one.

Sarah now responds to her father, demonstrating her name sign.

  • Right hand chin height. The ASL word “boy” has right hand at forehead height, representing the hat that a man would take off when meeting a woman. Sign names and relationship words use this right hand at forehead location to represent “maleness,” like Father or Uncle. The ASL word “girl” has right hand at chin / cheek height, possibly representing the bonnet strings that a girl would tie under her chin. Sign names and relationship words use this right hand at chin height to represent “femaleness,”, like Mother, Aunt, and Sarah’s name sign.
  • Right palm out. Initialized ASL words tend to have the palm facing out to make it easy to see which manual alphabet letter is being used.
  • Right zero fingers extended representing the letter S that starts Sarah’s name.
  • Right hand move left. She bounces her hand on her cheek twice. ASL words that have a single, broad movement tend to be verbs. Words with a shorter, repeated movement tend to be nouns.

Ken turns his body back toward the audience to clearly restate his guest’s sign name.

Turning his body back to Sarah, Ken asks for her age. Instead of stating the correct word, he makes a joke by using an ASL rhyming word (one parameter off.)

ASL word “Age” implies pulling down on a long beard.

  • Right hand chin height. Obviously where the beard starts.
  • Right palm neutral. This is how you would grab a beard from the side.
  • Right zero fingers extended. Ken uses a claw hand with all fingers bent to grab the beard, then changes to a fist at the bottom of the beard. I just included the fist handshape for simplicity.
  • Move down. Ken repeats the movement to represent “an age number” instead of the verb “aging.”

Note that he is looking at the audience, but his sentence ends with his body facing Sarah. ASL speakers drift their gaze back and forth for variety. A sentence’s ending location is more important for determining who they are speaking to, similar to raising the tone of your voice at the end of a question in English.

The pun here is clear. Ken moves ASL word “Eleven” to his forehead when giving a suggestion for her age, giving the mispronunciation “Understand.” Sarah ignores her father’s sense of humor (fairly common in Deaf culture) and simply answers with the correct ASL word. Ken turns back to the audience and restates the correct ASL word with nodding appreciation for the correction.

Ken now sets up the goal of the interview. He will discuss his encounter with Sarah’s friends when he went to her school.

Still facing the audience, Ken uses the word “you” toward Sarah. ASL does not have pronouns like “you,” “he,” “she,” “it,” “him,” “her,” “they”, “them.” Pointing with a single finger is used with attention focused on a specific person or scene. This is enough context that the extra words from English are not needed.

ASL word for “me” is the same word for “I”. It is just the one extended finger pointing back at the speaker, the opposite of “you.” Also note that the joining word “And” is not usually used in ASL.

ASL word “Discuss” represents the concept of “back and forth.” The left hand palm up implies a piece of paper with writing that both parties need to agree on. The pointer finger is highlighting a certain sentence in the document that needs attention. Passing the paper back and forth means both people are identifying sentences they want to understand clearly.

ASL word “About” represents the concept of going off topic and getting back to what is important. The right hand’s one finger is circling around around something. The left hand’s palm is facing out. This represents a person that is standing up and stationary, the left finger being a kind of puppet. The right finger moves “about” this person and then points right at them.

Like ASL word “you,” ASL word “your” does not need the variation English has with “his,” “hers,” “its”, and “theirs.” The neutral handshape simply pushes something toward a person or scene, implying the thing being pushed is their responsibility.

ASL word “Friend” represents two items being hooked together. Joining relationships in ASL often has the fingers or hands grasping each other, like in “Join” or “Marriage.” Friend has right hand’s extended fingers cross over left hand’s. Then the right hand’s actions are mirrored by the left hand in switched positions with the left hand on top this time, implying the relationship is a two-way street.

English prepositions like in, on, around, above, below (anywhere a mouse can go) have ASL equivalents, but they are not usually used as linking words. ASL would normally use these words to visually place one scene within another scene. Ken is using ASL word “At” in a very English-like way. Normally, ASL speakers just use a palm up neutral hand and shove it toward a scene to indicate where something is “at.”

ASL word “School” represents a teacher clapping their hands loudly to get an entire class’ attention when many are distracted. Many common ASL words represent scenes encountered in school life or home life. There is no Deaf country, so there has not been any need for large groups of Deaf people to create a “professional” register like English. Deaf people frequently use phrases that seem informal to an English speaker. Deaf people do not usually need to be politically correct in how they speak in ASL because most people they would talk to are friends and family, not businesses or governments.
