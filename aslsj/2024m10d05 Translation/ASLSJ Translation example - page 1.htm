ASLSJ Translation Example

Ken Davis (Deaf Newspaper) interviewed his daughter, Sarah, after a visit to her class at school. It is a human-interest story that Ken wanted to present on his ASL news channel. The video is available on YouTube: An Interview with Sarah Davis — “Meet A Deaf Person”

I describe my ASLSJ transcription over the next several articles

The video starts with Ken introducing himself. The first sentence is translated as “Hello. Welcome to our Deaf Newspaper.” Although ASL can put words in a different order than English, many Deaf people tend to keep their sentences in English word order because they are used to reading and writing in English.

Here, Ken’s actual words are, “Hello. Welcome our Deaf Newspaper.” ASL does not use linking words like “to” because the speaker can just change where they are looking to indicate a link. Therefore, not all words in ASL are on the hands. It is important to pay attention to their facial expressions and the “scenes” they have setup in previous sentences.

ASL has a lot of nuance for native speakers. For these examples, I omit a lot of details just to establish a basic understanding of how to understand ASLSJ writing.

The five basic parts of meaning in an ASL word are:

  1. The height of the hands: forehead, chin, and chest area.
  2. The location of the dominant hand relative to the non-dominant hand: up, down, left, right, out, in, or one-handed.
  3. The way a hand’s fingers are pointing and its palm is facing: out, in, or sideways.
  4. How many fingers are extended on a hand: none, 1, 2, 3, or 4
  5. What movement happens with a hand: up, down, left, right, out, in, twist at the wrist, bend or flick the fingers, or some combination.

These basic building blocks combine to create a language as varied as any spoken language. Although ASL words are understandable whether you consistently use your right hand or your left hand, most people use their right hand as their dominant hand. The rest of my examples will say “right hand” or “right side” for convenience, but you can translate these to ‘left hand” and “left side” if you use your left hand as your dominant hand.

The ASL word for “Hello” is similar to waving your hand at someone to say Hello. ASL’s word has the following parts:

  • The right hand starts on the forehead. The above drawing shows a head, neck and shoulders with a line on crossing the forehead. The small circle on the right shoulder shows which side of the drawing the right hand is on.
  • Since this is a one-handed ASL word, there is no relative placement of the right hand from the left hand. This part is left out.
  • The right hand’s palm is facing left. This is a neutral position for the wrist, just like you would have when you drop your arms to your side. The drawing above is supposed to look like there is a dot in the palm of your hand, but you are looking at the thin side of the hand. The bottom of the triangle is where your thumb would be.
  • The right hand has four fingers extended. This is the neutral handshape for most ASL words unless the number of fingers extended has an additional meaning. This drawing shows the thumb extended, but the placement of the thumb is not actually that for ASL words.
  • The right hand moves out, away from the body. ASLSJ uses arrows for up, down, left, and right directions. I use stacked triangles pointing up or down to represent an arrow on the floor. When the triangles are pointing up, the arrow points away from your feet. The small circle on the right of the triangles which side of the drawing the right hand is on.

Put these four parts together, and you make the ASL word Hello. Although there might be a reason why these parts are used, there is no definitive history for most ASL words. Most people come up their own reasoning for why certain parts could make sense to them so to help them remember how to make the word later.

The word Welcome looks like you are motioning someone to come into your home.

  • The right hand is in front of the chest. This is the neutral location for most ASL words. ASL speakers don’t stare at each other’s hands, but look at the other’s face. ASL words can be made in front of the stomach, but it might be a little low to be seen in your peripheral vision in a normal conversation. Therefore, most of the time you will raise your hands slightly higher than the stomach area. The drawing above shows the shoulders curving down around the outline of your arms. The small circle on the right side shows which side of the drawing the right hand is on.
  • The right hand’s palm is facing left. (neutral wrist)
  • The right hand has four fingers extended. (neutral handshape)
  • The right hand moves inward while also twisting the wrist. The stacked triangle pointing down is like an arrow on the floor pointing at your feet, which implies the right hand is moving in toward your body. The capital S just represents twisting the hand at the wrist. The small circles are placed on all locations and movements to identify where your right side is in the drawings.
  • The right hand palm ends facing inward or up. Most ASL words are not confused with each other when you are slightly off on one or two parts of the word. To keep things simple, I will treat “palm facing in” and “palm facing up” as having the same drawing. The drawing above represents the fingers as a flat line, the thumb as a curve on the side of the hand, and a dot in the middle of your palm. As the right hand palm is facing in, you can see the dot.

Next, Ken says to where you are being welcomed. He chose Deaf Newspaper as his YouTube personality. He says the channel is “our” Deaf Newspaper, implying the Deaf community has been important in establishing his channel.

  • ASL word “our” starts in the neutral location (chest height), neutral palm facing (left), and neutral handshape (four fingers extended).
  • The right hand moves left while twisting at the wrist.
  • The right hand ends with palm facing in or up.

“Our” is part of a group of ASL words that form some kind of circle to identify a group of people. Other ASL words like “class” and “them” form a circle away from the body. “Our” and “us” words form the circle on the body, implying that the speaker is part of this group.

There are several ways of saying the word Deaf in ASL, each with regional variation or extra context. Ken uses the most common word I know, which points at the side of the mouth and then the ear. As Deaf people do not hear, they rarely learn how to speak well. This ASL word “Does not speak, does not listen” means someone is culturally Deaf and not just a hearing person who went deaf.

  • The right hand is at the chin or mouth height. The exact placement is not very important for understanding what the word means.
  • The right hand is facing out or down. The drawing above shows a straight line for the fingers, a hook for the thumb, and two lines on the back of the hand representing the tendons that stick out when your fingers are extended.
  • Right hand has only one finger extended. In this word, it is simply a pointing finger.
  • Right hand moves in. The motion is more like a tap on the chin, bounce out, then tap on the ear, but the inward motion is close enough to understand in writing form.

ASL word “Newspaper” uses two hands. The left hand represents a piece of paper, and the right hand is something like a typewriter key striking the paper to imprint a letter in ink.

  • Right hand neutral location
  • In the drawing above after the location, the tilted half-circle represents the right hand. The two lines below the right hand means the right hand is above the left hand, not touching. The small circle shows where your right side is relative to the drawing.
  • Right hand palm out (you can see the tendons on the back of your hand), thumb on left side of the right hand.
  • Right hand one finger extended (a typewriter key’s arm ready to strike the paper)
  • Left hand palm in or up (you can see the dot on the palm), thumb on the left side of the left hand. The line under the second palm-facing drawing means this is the left hand’s palm facing direction.
  • Left hand neutral handshape. The left hand’s handshape also has a line under it.
  • Right hand move down. In the video, Ken repeats the movement. Sometimes repetition or a bouncing movement is important to tell certain ASL words apart. I am not including the repetition part of the movement right now to keep things simple.

Although there are a lot of parts in Ken’s first sentence, these parts are re-used in different combination to create all other ASL words. The next article will continue with transcribing the video’s ASL sentences.
